# Chart of Accounts (COA)

This page outlines the LLC chart of accounts and provides a summary of the workflow used for tracking business activities. 

[IRS Rental Property Doc](https://www.irs.gov/publications/p527)

## Chart of Accounts (COA)

The following shows the set of accounts for the multi-unit rental property & management LLC
This serves as the "index" for all financial activity and is identified by 4 categories

#### 1. Assets (1000-1999)
These are what your LLC owns. For multi-unit properties, it is critical to track both liquid cash and the long-term value of the buildings. 

- 1010 **Acct.Cash.Bank** : Cash in Bank (Operating Account)
- 1020 **Acct.Cash.Security** 1: Security Deposit Trust Account (Funds held for tenants; must be kept separate)
- 1410 **Acct.Fixed.Land**: Land
- 1420 **Acct.Fixed.Tangible**: Buildings (The purchase price of the physical structure)
- 1430 **Acct.Fixed.Depreciation.Accum**: Accumulated Depreciation (A "contra-asset" account tracking the building's value loss over time) 

#### 2. Liabilities (2000-2999) 
These are what your LLC owes to others. 

- 2010 **Acct.Liab.Payable**: Accounts Payable (Unpaid bills to vendors)
- 2100 **Acct.Liab.Morgage**: Mortgage Payable (The principal balance on your property loan)
- 2200 **Acct.Liab.Customer.Security**: Tenant Security Deposits Held (This offsets the cash in your trust account)
- 2300 **Acct.Liab.Customer.Rent.Prepai**: Prepaid Rent (Rent paid by tenants before it is due, first, last) 

#### 3. Equity (3000-3999)
This represents the owner's stake in the LLC. 

- 3010 **Acct.Equity.Owner.Cash**: Owner’s Contribution (Personal funds you put into the business)
- 3020 **Acct.Equity.Owner.Dist**: Owner’s Distribution (Money you take out of the business)
- 3025 **Acct.Equity.Owner.Draw**: Owner’s Draw (Money you take out of the business)
- 3100 **Acct.Equity.Earnings.PnL**: Retained Earnings (Cumulative profit or loss from previous years) 

#### 4. Income (4000-4999)
Revenue generated by the property. For multi-unit tracking, many bookkeepers use sub-accounts (e.g., 4010.01 for Unit 1, 4010.02 for Unit 2) to monitor performance by unit. 

- 4010 **Acct.Rev.OwnerRent**: Rental Income
- 4020 **Acct.Rev.OwnerFees**: Late Fee Income
- 4030 **Acct.Rev.Services** : Laundry/Vending Income
- 4040 **Acct.Rev.Other**: Pet Fees/Parking Fees
- 4050 **Acct.Rev.Util** : Utility Reimbursement (If tenants pay you back for shared utilities) 

#### 5. Expenses (5000-5999) 
These should ideally mirror IRS Schedule E categories to make tax season seamless. 

- 5010 **Acct.Exp.Adv**: Advertising
- 5020 **Acct.Exp.Maint**: Cleaning and Maintenance
- 5030 **Acct.Exp.Ins**: Insurance
- 5040 **Acct.Exp.Fees.Legal**: Legal & Professional Fees (Accountants, lawyers)
- 5050 **Acct.Exp.Fees.Mgmt**: Management Fees (If using a third-party manager)
- 5060 **Acct.Exp.Int.Morg**: Mortgage Interest (Interest only; principal goes to Liability 2100)
- 5070 **Acct.Exp.Repair**: Repairs (Routine fixes like leaky faucets)
- 5080 **Acct.Exp.Tax.Prop**: Taxes (Property taxes)
- 5090 **Acct.Exp.Util**: Utilities (Water, trash, common area electric) 


## Workflow

To record transactions for a rental property LLC, you must follow a structured cycle that moves from individual daily events to high-level financial reports. As a bookkeeper, your primary objective is to maintain the Accounting Equation: 

#### . Recording Rental Transactions
- **Separate Finances**: Open a dedicated business bank account for the LLC to avoid "commingling" funds, which is essential for maintaining the LLC's limited liability protection.
Identify Income: Record all rental revenue, including monthly rent, late fees, and non-refundable deposits.
- **Categorize Expenses**: Track operating expenses separately from capital expenditures (e.g., a new HVAC unit), as the latter must be depreciated over time rather than deducted immediately.
- **Manage Security Deposits**: Record deposits as a liability on the balance sheet, not as income, since they are typically owed back to the tenant.
- **Capture Mortgage Components**: Split mortgage payments into two parts: the interest (an expense) and the principal (a reduction of your loan liability). 


#### 2. How Accounts, Journals, and Ledgers Relate
The bookkeeping process follows a specific hierarchy to ensure every dollar is accounted for: 
Chart of Accounts (COA): This is your organizational "index" or master list of every category you use (e.g., Rent Income, Repairs, Cash, Mortgage Payable).
Journal (The Diary): Transactions are first entered here in chronological order. Each entry uses double-entry bookkeeping, where at least one account is debited and another is credited to keep the books balanced.
General Ledger (The Summary): Information from the journal is "posted" to the ledger. While the journal is chronological, the ledger is organized by account. It shows the running balance and total activity for each category in your COA.
Bank Statements (The Proof): These serve as external verification for your internal records. You must perform monthly bank reconciliations to ensure the ending balance in your ledger matches the ending balance on your bank statement.

## Line of Credit by LLC

- An LLC can offer a line of credit backed by a member's funds - called `owner loan`/`line of credit` w/ member.
- This process requires a formal loan agreement
    - a promissory note to document the terms
    - interest rate
    - repayment schedule
    - IRS may reclassify the transaction as a taxable distribution (if not properly documented, and
    - other members must approve

### How to set up the line of credit

- Create a formal loan agreement
- legally enforceable promissory note or master loan agreement.
- essential terms:
    - The agreement should outline the total amount,
    - interest rate (which should be at least the Applicable Federal Rate),
    - repayment schedule,
    - and other loan terms.
- Member approval: If there are other members, the loan must be approved by all of them.
- proper procedures to ensure the transaction is recognized as a loan and not a personal expense or a distribution.
- Document the loan: Keep detailed records of the loan and its repayment to avoid potential tax issues.
- Consider tax implications: The loan may have tax implications,
    - especially if the terms are not properly documented
    - if the loan is forgiven. 
- Personal risk: you are personally liable if the borrower - is unable to repay the debt.
- Business credit: A line of credit from a member does not build business credit with external lenders vs traditional loan.
- Lender requirements: Lenders may still require a personal guarantee from the member
    - mitigate their risk even with an established LLC.
 
### Consideration

- **Loan guarantee**:
    - A member can personally guarantee a loan for the LLC,
    - member(s) are personally responsible for repayment if the LLC defaults
- **Operating agreement**:
    - The operating agreement should specify who is authorized to guarantee loans
    - who has access to the line of credit.
- **Third-party lenders**:
    - If the LLC is seeking a traditional line of credit,
    - lenders will often require a personal guarantee from any member
    - a significant ownership stake
- **Member loans**:
    - member can also provide a loan directly to the LLC,
    - requires a formal loan agreement with specified terms (interest rate, payment schedule)
- **Tax implications**:
    - The tax treatment of member loans can be complex.
    - It's crucial to have proper documentation
    - have arm's-length transaction to avoid penalties from the IRS
- **Professional advice**:
    - Consult with a legal and tax professional
    - ensure the loan is structured correctly
    - complies with all applicable laws and regulations. 
 
### Difference Between a Loan and an Investment?

- An investment into your business is called a `capital contribution`

**capital contribution**
- is basically you putting some of your personal money on the line in the corporation.
- The funds become BUSINESS equity.
- Pay capital gains tax if you withdraw the investment in the future. 

**loan**
- money given to the BUSINESS with the intention of it being repaid, usually with interest.
- create a detailed written loan agreement
    - make sure both parties understand the terms (like repayment terms and interest rates).
    - The loan must be an “arms-length transaction,”
    - it treats you and the business as completely separate entities.

## Tax Benefits of Loaning Money to a Business?
There can be tax benefits to loaning money to a business, but these benefits often depend on the structure of the loan and the specific circumstances. Here are a few potential tax advantages associated with lending money to a business:

### Interest income: 
If you charge the business interest on the loan, the interest income you receive is generally taxable as ordinary income. This income can be a source of revenue and is typically taxed at your regular income tax rate. It’s important to keep accurate records of the interest earned.

### Tax deductions: 
On the business side, the interest payments made to you as the lender are typically tax-deductible for the business. This can reduce the company’s taxable income, resulting in lower tax liability. However, there are rules and limitations, so it’s important to consult with a tax professional to ensure compliance.

### Bad debt deductions: 
If the business defaults on the loan and is unable to repay it, you may be able to claim a bad debt deduction. This can offset some of your taxable income, but there are specific criteria that must be met for a debt to be considered “bad.”

### Capital losses: 
If you sell the debt at a loss, you may be able to claim a capital loss. This loss can be used to offset capital gains, potentially reducing your overall tax liability. The rules surrounding capital losses can be complex, so it’s wise to seek professional advice.

## Self-directed Solo 401(k) with LLC

To create an LLC with a Solo 401(k), first establish the LLC, obtain its Employer Identification Number (EIN), and then create the Solo 401(k) plan through a provider, which will require a separate EIN for the trust. The next steps are to open bank and brokerage accounts for the 401(k) trust and begin contributing funds from the business. 

1. You establish a self-directed Solo 401(k)
1. you register a single-member LLC. The Solo 401(k) becomes the sole member of the LLC
2. invests its funds into the business.

### Tax implications: 
- As long as the funds remain within the qualified plan,
- no taxes are incurred.
- The Solo 401(k) can invest in various assets through the LLC.
- This method requires `careful setup`, including
    - an operating agreement and
    - EIN for the LLC, and f
    - funding the LLC from the Solo 401(k) account. 

### Must avoid

- **Direct withdrawal**:
    - Do not cash out the 401(k) and deposit the money into an LLC's bank account.
    - This is considered a distribution and will be subject to income tax and a 10% early withdrawal penalty if you are under age 59½.
- **Indirect rollover**:
    - Avoid taking a check from your old 401(k) and depositing it into a new account yourself.
    - You will have to pay a mandatory 20% withholding and
    - must deposit the full amount into a new qualified plan within 60 days to avoid taxes and penalties. 

### How to proceed
1. establish a Solo 401(k) and a single-member LLC for the other method.
2. Roll over funds: Transfer the money from your old 401(k) into the new qualified plan via a direct rollover.
3. This is a non-taxable event.
4. Invest the funds: The new 401(k) plan will then invest in the business's stock (for ROBS) or in the LLC's assets.

### 1. Establish your LLC
Choose a unique name for your LLC and file the necessary paperwork with your state to form the business entity.
If you plan to use the LLC for investments, consider creating a "special purpose" LLC where the Solo 401(k) trust is the sole member, as explained in this article from My Solo 401k Financial. 
### 2. Get your EIN and set up the Solo 401(k) plan
Obtain an Employer Identification Number (EIN) for your LLC from the IRS.
Choose a Solo 401(k) provider and complete their application and adoption agreement to formally set up the plan.
The provider will help you create a separate EIN for the Solo 401(k) trust, which is necessary to open the plan's accounts. 
### 3. Open accounts and contribute funds
Using the Solo 401(k) trust's EIN, open bank and brokerage accounts in the name of the trust.
You can now begin contributing to the plan. Your contributions are made from your business's net income and can be made through employee and employer contributions.

### Rollover
- You can also roll over funds from a former employer's 401(k) or IRA into your new Solo 401(k) plan. 
- Important considerations
- No full-time employees: A Solo 401(k) can only be established if there are no full-time employees other than you and your spouse.
- Contribution limits: The amount you can contribute depends on your net self-employment income and is subject to annual IRS limits.
- Trustee: You will need to designate a trustee for the 401(k) plan.
    - This can be yourself, or yourself and a spouse if applicable.
    - LLC operating agreement:
        - Your LLC's operating agreement may need specific language to maintain compliance,
        - particularly if the LLC will be used for investments.

## Asset-Based Loans (`ABL`)
Issuing a loan is based on the present, appraised value of an asset used as collateral, not its potential future value. This practice is known as asset-based lending (ABL). Lenders use the asset's current value to mitigate their risk; if you default, they seize and sell the asset to recover their losses. 

### Collateral is Key: 
ABL involves securing a loan with tangible assets like 
- accounts receivable,
- inventory, equipment, or
- real estate.

### Loan-to-Value (LTV) Ratio: 
- Lenders determine the maximum loan amount using a loan-to-value (LTV) ratio.
- This ratio `loan amount` / `current appraised value` (Or Sale Price @ time of closing)

### Risk Mitigation: 
- The more liquid an asset (the easier it is to convert to cash), the higher the LTV ratio a lender may offer.
- Highly liquid assets (stocks, bonds) may receive an LTV of 50% to 70% or more.
- Physical assets (inventory, equipment) are considered riskier and may only receive an LTV of around 50%.

### Recourse in Default: 
- If the borrower fails to make payments, the lender has the legal right to seize the collateral
    - Lender may sell it to recoup their funds. 

### Common Types of Asset-Based Loans
- **Securities-Based Line of Credit (SBLOC)**: Borrowing against the value of your investment portfolio without selling the underlying investments.
- **Home Equity Line of Credit (HELOC) or Cash-out Refinance**: Leveraging the current equity in your real estate property.
- **Accounts Receivable Financing**: Using a business's outstanding invoices as collateral for immediate cash flow.

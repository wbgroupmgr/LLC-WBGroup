# LLC (3 Members) IRS Tax Example: F1065 & SchK-1

This example demonstrates the tax reporting for "ABC Consulting LLC," a 3-member LLC taxed as a partnership, 
using Form 1065 and Schedule K-1. 


## 1. Scenario Overview

- LLC Name: ABC Consulting LLC
- Ownership:
    - Partner A (40%),
    - Partner B (35%),
    - Partner C (25%)
- Financials:
    - $300,000 : Gross Revenue: 
    - $100,000 : Expenses (Rent, Supplies):
    - $200,000 : `Net Ordinary Income` (Line 1):
- Distribution:
    - 60K, 52.5K, 37.5K : Total cash distribution of was made  respectively 

## 2. Form 1065 - U.S. Return of Partnership Income 

- This form summarizes the total activity of the LLC. 
- Header:
    - ABC Consulting LLC,
    - Employer Identification Number (EIN),
    - Date business started.
    - Item G:
        - Check "Initial return," "Final return," "Name change," or "Address change" if applicable.
    - Item H: Check "Cash" or "Accrual" (usually Cash for small businesses).
    - Item I: Number of Schedules K-1 (Enter 3).
- Income (Page 1):
    - $300,000 : Line 1a: Gross receipts or sales
    - $300,000 : Line 8_: Total Income
    - $100,000 : Line 20: Total expenses (Rent, Salaries, etc.)
    - $200,000 : Line 22: Net Ordinary Business Income
- Schedule K (Page 4):
    - $200,000 : Line 1: Ordinary business income
    - Line 14: Self-Employment Earnings (if `active members`). 

## 3. Schedule K-1 (Form 1065) Examples
A separate K-1 must be filled out for each of the three partners. 

#### K-1 for Partner A (40% Owner) 
- Part I (Partnership Info): ABC Consulting LLC, EIN, Address.
- Part II (Partner Info):
    - Partner A Name, Address, SSN.
    - Item J (Profit/Loss/Capital %): Beginning: 40% / Ending: 40%
    - Item K: Check "General Partner" or "Limited Liability Company Member."
- Part III (Partner's Share):
    - 80K : Box 1 (Ordinary Income) - 200K * 40%
    - 80K : Box 14 (Self-Employment) (Code A - Net earnings)
    - 60K : Box 19 (Distributions) (Cash received) 

#### K-1 for Partner B (35% Owner) 
- Part II (Partner Info): Partner B Name, Address, SSN.
    - Item J: Beginning: 35% / Ending: 35%
- Part III (Partner's Share):
    - 70K : Box 1 (Ordinary Income)
    - 70K : Box 14 (Self-Employment)
    - 52.2K : Box 19 (Distributions)

#### K-1 for Partner C (25% Owner) 
- Part II (Partner Info): Partner C Name, Address, SSN.
    - Item J: Beginning: 25% / Ending: 25%
- Part III (Partner's Share):
    - 50K : Box 1 (Ordinary Income)
    - 50K :  Box 14 (Self-Employment)
    - 37,5K : Box 19 (Distributions) 

#### Key Takeaways for 3-Member LLCs

- Pass-Through Entity:
    - The LLC does not pay income tax on the Net Income $200,000.
    - Instead, partners report the income from their K-1 on their personal Form 1040.
- Self-Employment Tax:
    - LLC members usually pay self-employment tax on their share of ordinary income (Box 14).
- Distributions vs. Income:
    - Partners are taxed on their share of income (80K / 70K / 50k)
    - not the cash they took out (60K / 52.5k / 37.5K)
- Filing Deadline:
    - Form 1065 is due on the 15th day of the 3rd month after the tax year ends (March 15th for calendar year partnerships).
 
## Re-investing Distributions into the LLC

- an LLC member can elect to leave their share of profits in the LLC
    - rather than withdrawing them.
    - done to reinvest in the business, improve cash flow, or fund future development. 
- leaving money in the LLC does not change the tax liability for that income. 
- **Pass-Through Taxation**:
    - LLCs are generally taxed as `pass-through entities`
    - `disregarded entities` for single-member LLCs).
    - IRS taxes the member on the profit **earned** by the business
    - IRS does not the money distributed to the member.
- **Taxed on Undistributed Earnings**:
    - Member leaves their distribution in the LLC
    - 100K profit : generated by LLC
    - Member is still taxed on that $100,000 in your personal tax return.
- **Self-Employment Tax**:
    - You must pay self-employment tax (Social Security and Medicare) on the net earnings of the LLC
    - regardless of whether you withdraw the money.
- **Basis Increase**:
    - Leaving profits in LLC increase `basis` (or investment stake)
- **Exceptions and Considerations**:
    - LLC Taxed as Corporation:
        - LLC has elected to be taxed as a C Corporation or S Corporation,
        - the tax rules change
        - taxes may only be owed when money is actually distributed or paid as salary.
- **Operating Agreement**:
    - The ability to retain earnings should be outlined in the LLC's operating agreement
    - Ensure all members are in agreement.
